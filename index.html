Equipment Guide

What You Need to Get Started

Volume 1 covers every piece of equipment in detail. Here's the overview so you know what you're getting into before you buy a single thing.

Torch

An oxygen-propane bench torch is your primary tool. Beginner-friendly options include the Bethlehem Alpha and GTT Bobcat — both handle borosilicate glass and run on oxygen concentrators to save money.

Safety Glasses

Didymium glasses that block infrared, UV, and sodium flare. Non-negotiable — your eyes need protection from the torch's intense light and radiation. Covered in detail in Volume 1.

Regulators & Hoses

Oxygen and fuel regulators control gas pressure to your torch. T-grade hoses with B fittings and flashback arrestors keep you safe. Your gas setup is the foundation of your studio.

Hand Tools

Graphite paddles, tungsten probes, tweezers (8", 10", 12"), glass nippers, scoring knife, claw grabbers, and graphite rods. These shape your molten glass into finished art.

Glass & Color

Clear borosilicate tubing (COE 33) as your base, plus color rods for decoration and fuming metals — silver and gold — for creating the signature color-changing effect.

Annealing Kiln

A kiln slowly cools your finished glass to relieve internal stress. Without proper annealing, your work cracks. Borosilicate is held at 1050°F and cooled at 1–2 degrees per minute.

Color-changing glass is created through a technique called fuming. You vaporize precious metals — silver and gold — in front of your torch flame. The metal fumes bond to the borosilicate glass surface, creating an iridescent effect that becomes visible as the inside of the pipe darkens with use. Silver creates blue, purple, and yellow tones. Gold produces pink, green, and orange hues. Six of our eight courses teach fuming techniques at progressive skill levels.

Borosilicate glass (also called "boro" or "hard glass") contains boron trioxide, giving it superior thermal shock resistance and durability compared to soda-lime "soft" glass. Boro requires higher working temperatures but produces stronger, longer-lasting finished pieces. It's the preferred material for functional glass pipes, scientific equipment, and high-quality art glass. Pyrex is the most well-known borosilicate brand.
